Neptune

Neptune—the eighth and most distant major planet orbiting our Sun—is dark, cold and whipped by supersonic winds. It was the first planet located through mathematical calculations.

Neptune, the ice giant planet located on the outermost edge of our solar system, is a dark and frigid world buffeted by fierce supersonic winds. It is invisible to the naked eye and can only be observed through telescopes. It takes Neptune 165 years to complete one orbit around the sun, a journey it has made only once since its discovery in 1846. Because of its great distance from the sun, the brightest point of the day on Neptune would be comparable to twilight on Earth, and the sunlight on our planet is almost 900 times brighter than that experienced on Neptune.

Namesake:

Through mathematical calculations, Neptune was the initial planet to be discovered. In 1846, Johann Galle found the planet based on predictions made by Urbain Le Verrier. Le Verrier proposed the name Neptune, after the Roman god of the sea.

Potential for life:

The conditions on Neptune make it unsuitable for life as we understand it. The atmosphere, temperature, and substances found on this planet are probably too intense and unstable for living beings to survive.

Size and distance:

Neptune is much larger than Earth, with a radius of 15,299.4 miles (24,622 kilometers) and a size comparable to a baseball if Earth were a nickel. It is also 30 astronomical units away from the Sun, which is 2.8 billion miles (4.5 billion kilometers) on average. Sunlight takes four hours to reach Neptune from this distance.

Orbit and its rotation:

A full rotation of Neptune, or a day on the planet, takes around 16 hours. A year on Neptune, which is the time it takes for the planet to make a complete orbit around the Sun, is equivalent to about 165 Earth years or 60,190 Earth days. During a 20-year period every 248 Earth years, Pluto's orbit brings it inside Neptune's orbit, making it closer to the Sun than Neptune. This last occurred between 1979 and 1999. However, Pluto cannot collide with Neptune due to their different orbit patterns. Neptune's axis of rotation is tilted 28 degrees, allowing it to experience seasons that last over 40 years each, similar to Mars and Earth.

Number of Moons:

There are 14 known moons that orbit Neptune. The biggest one, Triton, was found by William Lassell on October 10, 1846, just 17 days after Johann Gottfried Galle discovered the planet. As Neptune is named after the Roman god of the sea, its moons are named after lesser sea gods and nymphs from Greek mythology. Triton is unique because it is the only large moon in the solar system that orbits its planet in the opposite direction to the planet's rotation. This suggests that it may have been an independent object that Neptune captured. Triton is extremely cold, with surface temperatures of around minus 391 degrees Fahrenheit (minus 235 degrees Celsius). Despite this, Voyager 2 discovered geysers that shoot icy material upward more than 5 miles (8 kilometers) from Triton's surface. The spacecraft also detected Triton's thin atmosphere, which has been observed from Earth several times since. Scientists are unsure why the atmosphere is growing warmer.

Rings:

Neptune is known to have five main rings and four noticeable ring arcs. These rings are called Galle, Leverrier, Lassell, Arago, and Adams and are believed to be relatively young and short-lived. Neptune's ring system also features peculiar clumps of dust known as arcs. The outermost ring, Adams, contains four prominent arcs named Liberté (Liberty), Egalité (Equality), Fraternité (Fraternity), and Courage. These arcs are strange because they should spread out evenly, but they remain clumped together. Scientists believe this is due to the gravitational effects of the nearby moon, Galatea.

Formation:

About 4.5 billion years ago, the formation of the solar system resulted in the creation of Neptune through the gravitational attraction of swirling gas and dust, ultimately forming an ice giant. It is believed that Neptune, like Uranus, was originally formed closer to the Sun but migrated to the outer reaches of the solar system about 4 billion years ago.

Structure:

Neptune, along with Uranus, is one of the two ice giants located in the outer region of our solar system. The planet's mass is primarily composed of a dense fluid of icy materials such as water, methane, and ammonia, with a small rocky core at the center. Compared to other giant planets, Neptune is the densest. According to scientists, there is a possibility of the presence of a scorching water ocean beneath the planet's cold clouds. The intense pressure prevents it from evaporating, and it remains trapped within.

Surface:

Unlike Earth, Neptune does not have a solid surface. The planet's atmosphere, mainly composed of hydrogen, helium, and methane, stretches to significant depths and eventually blends into water and other melted ices. Beneath this atmosphere lies a dense, solid core that weighs about the same as Earth.



Atmosphere:

The atmosphere of Neptune is mainly composed of hydrogen and helium, with a small amount of methane. Uranus, Neptune's neighboring planet, appears blue-green due to the presence of atmospheric methane. However, Neptune's bright and vivid blue color suggests that there may be another unknown component causing the intense hue. Despite being the farthest planet from the Sun with low energy input, Neptune is the windiest planet in our solar system. Its winds are three times stronger than Jupiter's and nine times stronger than Earth's. These powerful winds move clouds of frozen methane at speeds over 1,200 miles per hour. In 1989, a massive storm named the "Great Dark Spot" was discovered in Neptune's southern hemisphere. Although this storm has disappeared, new ones have formed in different parts of the planet.

Magnetosphere:

Neptune's magnetic field is not aligned with its rotation axis, instead being tipped over by approximately 47 degrees. This misalignment causes Neptune's magnetosphere to undergo erratic changes during each rotation, similar to Uranus which has a magnetic axis tilted around 60 degrees from its rotation axis. The magnetic field of Neptune is much stronger than that of Earth, being about 27 times more powerful.
